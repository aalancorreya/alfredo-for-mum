Mum's Anglo-Indian Oxtail/Valadi Soup

The greenish-brown one — the way you used to make it on cold nights.

Serves 4 Prep 15 min Cook 35 min

Mum — this is the greenish-brown one. I asked you once, when I was small, why it wasn't red like the soup at other people's houses, and I remember thinking it must be a herb that turned the broth that colour. You said no, it's the whole spices and the green chilli and the curry leaves and the turmeric, and never tomato. I've been carrying that answer around for twenty-odd years. I'm writing it down now so the next time someone asks me, I don't have to ring you to check.

Valadi means the bottom of the tail — oxtail — in the Tamil-Malayalam patois we grew up on, and you'll know the dish the second you read the name. This isn't the tomato-heavy English oxtail soup people think of when you say it in Australia. It's the other one: peppery, lightly spiced, sharp with curry leaf and clean down the back of the throat.

What goes in

Ingredients

Two parts: the cooker (everything spiced and simmered) and the tadka (fried at the very end and stirred through). Whole spices, not ground.

First, the cook

The three pans

Toss the meat with the aromatics, pressure cook with the whole spices, then make the tadka while the cooker rests.

1

Toss with the spices

In a steel bowl combine the oxtail with the crushed ginger, the crushed garlic, the slit green chillies, the turmeric and a good pinch of salt. Toss with your hands until every piece is properly coated — the turmeric should look uneven, that's fine, it'll bloom out as it cooks. Let it sit ten minutes if you have the time, or go straight to the cooker if you don't. The marinade is forgiving here; the heat does most of the work.

2

Pressure cook

Tip everything into a pressure cooker — the spiced oxtail, the cloves, the cardamom pods, the cinnamon stick, the peppercorns, the bay leaf if you're using one. Pour in water just to cover the meat, no more — the broth concentrates as it cooks and you don't want it thin. Lid on, weight on. One whistle on high heat — listen for it. The moment it blows, drop the heat to low and cook another ten to fifteen minutes. Take it off and let the pressure release naturally; opening early can leave the meat tough and the flavour flat.

3

The tadka

While the cooker rests, heat the ghee or oil in a small kadai. Slide in the sliced onion and the curry leaves — they'll spit and crackle, that's the point. Cook the onion through to a deep amber and the curry leaves until they're glossy and slightly crisp. Pull the pan off the heat, scatter in the pepper powder and (if you're using them) a pinch of turmeric and chilli flakes — off the heat, always, or the pepper goes bitter. Pour the entire pan, oil and all, into the open soup and stir through. Simmer briefly so the seasoning blends. Finish with garam masala on top.

Then, the tadka

The tadka, in four moves

This is the part that decides the soup. Small kadai, hot fat, off the heat for the powder spices — in that order, every time.

i Heat

Ghee or oil in a small kadai over medium-high. Let it shimmer before anything goes in — a cool pan turns the curry leaves limp instead of crisp, and the onion stews instead of browning.

ii Onion + curry leaves

Slide them in together. Onion through to a deep amber, leaves glossy and crisp at the edges. They'll spit and crackle — that's the point.

iii Off the heat

Always off the heat before the powder spices, or the pepper turns bitter. Scatter the pepper powder, an optional pinch of turmeric and chilli flakes.

iv Pour and finish

Tip the entire pan, oil and all, into the soup. Stir through. Finish with garam masala on top.

Bringing it together

Ladle into deep bowls. Spoon a piece of oxtail onto each one — the marrow is the prize, don't lose it to the bottom of the pot. Scatter a few extra fried curry leaves and a dust of garam masala over the top. Bread on the side, or rice when it's just you.

Curry leaves, garam masala, the pepper — the smell of your kitchen.

A small warning

whole spices, never pre-ground

The warmth in this soup comes from spices that were whole when they hit the pot. Pre-ground cloves, ground cardamom, ground cinnamon — they oxidise on the shelf, lose the volatile oils that carry the perfume, and what's left is dust. Buy whole. Crack the cardamom in your fingers as it goes in.

Pepper, coarse Freshly ground black pepper, coarse rather than fine — the heat is more honest, and you can see it sitting on the broth.
Cardamom Crack the green pods between your fingers as they go in — the seeds need to be exposed for the perfume to come out.
A note from me — A jar of whole spices keeps a year. A jar of pre-ground keeps a fortnight before it tastes like nothing.

Notes

The imperatives

  • One whistle, then low for ten to fifteen.
  • Always use whole spices.
  • Open the cooker only when it's fully released.
  • Tadka last, or the curry leaves go bitter.

Texture rescue

Broth too thin — simmer with the lid off ten minutes and it tightens up. Too peppery — a splash of cream or coconut milk softens it without dulling the spice.

How you know it's right

  • Colour: greenish-brown, never red.
  • Texture: meat slides off the bone with a spoon.
  • Heat: peppery on the back of the throat, not chilli-hot up front.
  • Salt: under rather than over — the broth tightens overnight.

Serve with

Bread, rice, or a hot mug on its own when the night turns cold.

The spoon lift — marrow, broth, a piece of the tail.

The kitchen smells the way your kitchen used to smell when the curry leaves hit the hot ghee — that's how I know I've done it right.
